should you build a 3d printer

I recently built a 3d printer from scratch! Okay, well I guess recently is maybe a stretch. This was a summer project while I interned at Stratasys. Frankly, being around all those 3D printers had me itching to tinker again.

My first exposure to 3d printing

I first learned about 3D printing firmly in the reprap era. I was pretty young when the OG mendel first entered open source (6 years old!). I think I first saw an SLA printer in an episode of popular mechanics and was absolutely entranced. It felt so futuristic! It wasn't until a few years later that I learned about companies like printrbot, who were making kits actually accessible.

Being a middle-schooler as all this unfolded, I had no means to participate in a material way. So instead, I would just pore over buildlogs. I remember being in my school's computer lab, longingly waiting for new posts from richRap (seriously, this guy was my idol). It wasn't until later in middle school, that I really considered getting into 3D printing for real. I saw two builds so inspiring that I knew I might actually have a chance to build one: ToyRep and Cherry. Both of these printers took advantage of cheap, geared stepper motors. They boast like, no torque, and have lots of backlash. But they cost pennies! Seeing these builds were so inspiring and a big part of what made me so enamored with the DIY printer movement.

In 8th grade, I saved up enough birthday money, and bothered my parents and grandparents enough to buy a 3D printer. It was a popular kit at the time, the makerfarm Pegasus 8". Makerfarm unfortunately no longer exists, but at the time there was a tight knit community around these machines. I recognize how insanely privileged I am; I'm so grateful that my family was able to afford this. It brought me so much joy, and legitimately changed the course of my life.

I put it all together, wired up a sketchy power supply, and started printing. This wasn't without it's challenges! Printers back then really lacked a lot of the modern sophistication they do now. You had 3 limit switches, a hotend, and some axes. Have fun! With such a barebones setup, I'd guess 50% of all my prints were just making improvements to the machine. At the time, I was using my student license of inventor to draft up brackets and gussets that helped keep the frame from shaking too much under high acceleration moves.

Those were the days. I don't want to give an exhaustive summary of my trials and tribulations with additive manufacturing here. We would be here awhile. I just wanted some amount of time to reminisce about where I started in this community.

Building a new printer

I've actually built several printers over the years. Though for the most part, I only ever had 2 operational printers at once. I had my original; the workpegasus workhorse that was the makerfarm, and whatever printer I was tinkering with at the time. It was too expensive to buy all new components (rails, motors, hotends, controllers) each time I wanted to experiment, so instead, I just took them apart and rebuilt them into something new. I built deltas, corexys, and of course the beloved bedslingers.

Then, I went to college. I quickly started showing up to our makerspace, and fixing 3d printers. That's actually how I got my job there; I just showed up, and started fixing up the monoprice sheetmetal i3 that they had. Like most 3d printers of this era, it was a high failure rate endeavor. You'd be constantly tightening belts, and releveling beds, and it felt like there was always something to be done to get things working well. During this time, with homework and a social life and work, it was hard to commit to making a whole new design. And so it became something I relegated to the back of my mind, hoping to return to someday.

The dawn of Mbu

The summer after my junior year, I was lucky enough to intern at Stratasys. It was a fantastic experience! Not only because I worked with a great team and got to make some cool projects, but also because I was geeking out the entire time about being in the birthplace (well, from a company perspective) of filament 3d printing. Every week or so, we'd tour some new facility or hear a talk from someone in the company about the business. Each time, I just got more of an itch to work on designing a printer again. That's how Mbu started! Mbu is the name of my folding 3d printer; it got this name because I was oddly obsessed with pufferfish at the time, and this printer was supposed to change it's size for travel.

Every night, I would drive home from the Stratasys corporate office, and plop down at my kitchen table to design this printer. I did it all in Fusion, and it was a really special experience. I felt like my younger self, pouring over buildlogs and researching designs. It had some quirks, given I was upholding my tradition of using recycled parts from printers past, but ultimately, it ended up being a pretty great little machine. I've made some updates here and there to the y-axis mounting system, but it's held up well.

The world is different now

With the advent of inexpensive Bambulabs printers, it really feels like the market has changed. 3D printing, up until this point, has always felt like a creature evolving in real time. You can watch as the years go by, and new features slowly outcompete old ones. That said, it always felt like progress moved in a line. Like despite these changes, some old quirks were still inherited from the past generation. You could still see the essence of the Mendel in the prusa mk3. Bambulabs really changed that. All of the sudden, it feels like we have a printer lineup that's totally disconnected from the hobbyist machines of old. It's obviously a little bittersweet. I love the origins of hobby 3d printing, and it pains me to see classic names pushed out of the market. But this community has always been about improvement, and there's a part of me that's equally excited that we have access to such powerful machines.

Building a 3d printer yourself is something that's become less and less necessary as the years go by. It used to be the case that you really could save money by DIYing! And if you're clever, you might even end up with a machine that outperforms the rest of the market. I think that era is mostly gone now though. Economies of scale have driven the price of premade printers so low, it's much more challenging to make something yourself and save money.

But let's be honest, that's not really why we're (okay, I) building 3d printers anymore. Just as it's a right of passage to reinvent the wheel with an http server in the software world, I think there's something equally special about designing a machine from scratch! It's a tremendous learning oppurtunity, a time honored tradition, and man does it feel good when your printer spits out it's first (ugly) benchy. Perhaps this isn't a very satisfying end; it's quite an obvious one. When you go to design your own machine, you're staring down countless teams of engineers who have all put their brains into making something that can survive the horrors of the market. Even still, you should challenge yourself! You should try to make something, even if it doesn't disrupt the market! If you want to push some plastic, by all means, buy a prusa. Buy a bambu. But if you want to learn, fire up your CAD of choice, and join in the RepRap dance.
